## Reflective Summary

This project was a first-phase empirical validation study in semi-supervised learning (SSL), conducted in collaboration with the founder of Lucidity Sciences and a Westminster faculty advisor. The research question is whether *Lumawarp*, a research-stage SSL engine the company is developing, can match the regularization quality of the classical manifold-regularization baseline (Belkin, Niyogi, and Sindhwani's 2006 LapRLSC) while reducing the per-iteration computational complexity from O(N³) toward O(N²) — which would make manifold-based SSL practical at the scale that real industrial tabular datasets demand. My role in Phase 1 was to reconstruct the baseline so that the comparison would be honest: I rebuilt LapRLSC on the canonical two-moons dataset in GNU Octave using the ManifoldLearn toolbox, replaced missing compiled MEX SVM solvers with a pure-Octave reformulation through `quadprog`, ran scaling experiments from N=200 to N=20,000, and used log-log regression on the timing data to empirically estimate the complexity class. Around that core, the project also included a substantial literature synthesis tying together Laplacian Eigenmaps, manifold regularization, kernel warping (Sindhwani et al.), joint-embedding SSL (Kiani et al.), and the Balestriero–LeCun unification of spectral embedding methods. The Phase-1 results — methodology, theoretical framing, and reproduction — were written up in a research poster and meeting digests, approved by the industry collaborator and faculty advisor, and accepted for presentation at the Westminster Student Showcase on May 1, 2026; Phase 2, which runs Lumawarp against the same benchmarks (including the tabarena tabular benchmark), is being deferred to a NeurIPS paper track.

I chose this project for my portfolio because it was the work that taught me what research actually feels like — not "implement the thing the textbook says," but reading several adjacent literatures until they line up, choosing what to reproduce versus what to take on faith, and defending those choices to people who know the area better than I do. That is where the Computer Science goals of software design and problem solving showed up in a form I had not encountered in coursework: the *design* decision was about experiment design and the comparison protocol, and the *problem solving* was figuring out how to keep the baseline honest after the MEX solvers turned out to be unavailable. The project most strongly addresses the Westminster goals of Critical Thinking (deciding when the baseline was trustworthy, and when scaling results were noise versus signal), Communication (the poster, methodology document, and weekly digests, all aimed at audiences with very different priors), Collaboration (working with both the Lucidity Sciences founder and the Westminster advisor, who had different — and sometimes conflicting — expectations about scope and rigor), and Global Responsibility, which is the goal this project speaks to most distinctively: scalable SSL matters precisely because the cost of labeled data falls disproportionately on under-resourced organizations and domains, and a method that lets practitioners learn from mostly-unlabeled data is a small but real lever on who gets to use machine learning at all.
